What Is R-Multiple in Trading? The Risk Unit That Unifies Every Metric

R-Multiple turns your stop loss into a unit of measurement. Once every trade is expressed as a multiple of your initial risk, win rate, expectancy, SQN, and Kelly Criterion all start speaking the same language.

Two traders both "made $500 today." One risked $100 to make it. The other risked $2,000. Those are not comparable results. The first trader made 5R. The second made 0.25R. One of these strategies is building a sustainable edge. The other is hoping big wins outpace eventual big losses. R-Multiple is what makes that distinction visible.

R-Multiple is not a new concept. Van Tharp popularized it in Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom, and it has been the organizing principle of professional systematic trading for decades. What's changed is that most retail traders still don't use it — which is exactly why their statistics lie to them.

Defining 1R: Your Risk Unit

Before you enter a trade, you place a stop loss. The dollar distance between your entry price and your stop — multiplied by your position size — is your initial risk in dollars. That is 1R.

// Example: EURUSD long
Entry: 1.0850
Stop Loss: 1.0820 // 30 pips
Position: 1 standard lot // $10/pip

1R = 30 pips × $10/pip = $300

Once 1R is defined at entry, every possible trade outcome becomes a multiple of it. The trade hits your 2:1 target? That's +2R. You exit early for half a loss? That's -0.5R. You're stopped out at full loss? That's -1R.

The R-Multiple Formula

R-Multiple = Trade P&L ($) ÷ Initial Risk ($)
Trade P&L Initial Risk (1R) R-Multiple
+$600 $300 +2.0R
+$150 $300 +0.5R
-$300 $300 -1.0R
-$150 $300 -0.5R
+$900 $300 +3.0R

Why R-Multiple Is the Foundation of Every Other Metric

Once you have a distribution of R-Multiples across your trades, every serious trading metric is computable. None of them require dollar amounts — only R values.

Expectancy

Trading expectancy is the average R-Multiple across all your trades — what you earn per trade, in R, on average.

Expectancy = (Win Rate × Avg Win R) − (Loss Rate × Avg Loss R)

A positive expectancy means you make money over time. A negative expectancy means you lose it. Win rate alone tells you neither — a 70% win rate with 0.3R average wins and 1.5R average losses is deeply unprofitable (+0.7×0.3 − 0.3×1.5 = −0.24R per trade).

SQN (System Quality Number)

SQN measures the consistency of your R-Multiple distribution, not just its average. A strategy can have high expectancy but wild variance — meaning it requires deep drawdowns to realize its edge. SQN penalizes that variance.

SQN = (Mean R × √N) ÷ Standard Deviation of R

Kelly Criterion

Kelly Criterion determines the mathematically optimal fraction of your account to risk per trade. It uses win rate and the ratio of average win R to average loss R — both derived from your R-Multiple history.

Kelly % = Win Rate − (Loss Rate ÷ Win-to-Loss R Ratio)

Win Rate (In Context)

Win rate alone is meaningless without knowing average win R and average loss R. R-Multiple provides that context. A 40% win rate with 3R wins and 1R losses (expectancy = +0.8R/trade) outperforms a 70% win rate with 0.5R wins and 2R losses (expectancy = −0.25R/trade). The win-rate-obsessed trader is losing money. The R-focused trader is building wealth.

How R-Multiple Works in Prop Firm Trading

Prop firm rules are risk-based: daily loss limits (typically 4–5% of account), max drawdown rules (typically 8–10%), and consistency requirements. These translate directly into R when you define 1R as a fixed percentage of funded account equity. A 5% daily loss limit becomes a "stop trading after -5R today" rule. Frequently Asked Questions

What is R-Multiple in trading?

R-Multiple is a way of measuring trade outcomes relative to your initial risk. Before you enter a trade, you define your stop loss — the maximum dollar amount you're willing to lose. That amount is 1R. If the trade wins twice that amount, the outcome is +2R. If it loses, it's -1R. R-Multiple converts every trade into a risk-normalized unit, so you can compare outcomes across different instruments, position sizes, and account sizes.

How do you calculate R-Multiple?

R-Multiple = Trade P&L ÷ Initial Risk (in dollars). If you risk $200 on a trade and it wins $500, the R-Multiple is +2.5R. If it loses $150 of a $200 stop, the R-Multiple is -0.75R. The key is that 1R is always defined at trade entry by your stop loss distance — not applied after the fact.

What is a good R-Multiple for a trade?

There is no universal 'good' R-Multiple in isolation — it depends on your win rate. A strategy with 40% win rate needs average winning trades above +2.5R to be profitable. What matters is expectancy: (Win Rate × Average Win R) − (Loss Rate × Average Loss R). A positive expectancy means the strategy earns money per trade on average.

How does R-Multiple relate to SQN?

SQN is calculated from your R-Multiple distribution: SQN = (Mean R × √N) ÷ Standard Deviation of R. R-Multiples are the raw input data. Without consistent R-Multiple tracking, you cannot calculate SQN. A higher mean R with lower standard deviation produces a better SQN score.

It's the foundation for expectancy, SQN, Kelly Criterion, and Monte Carlo simulation. Without normalizing trades to R, you can't meaningfully aggregate statistics across a multi-instrument journal.
